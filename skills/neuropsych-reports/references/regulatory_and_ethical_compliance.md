# Regulatory and Ethical Compliance for Neuropsychological Reports

Adapted from general clinical regulatory compliance standards, tailored for neuropsychological assessment documentation.

---

## HIPAA Compliance in Neuropsychological Practice

### Protected Health Information (PHI) in Neuropsychological Reports

Neuropsychological reports contain extensive PHI including cognitive test scores, psychiatric history, substance use, educational/occupational history, and diagnostic impressions. All reports must comply with HIPAA requirements.

### 18 HIPAA Identifiers

These identifiers must be removed for de-identified case examples, teaching materials, or research use:

1. **Names**
2. **Geographic subdivisions smaller than state** (except first 3 digits of ZIP if >20,000 people)
3. **Dates** (except year) — birth, evaluation, injury dates
4. **Telephone numbers**
5. **Fax numbers**
6. **Email addresses**
7. **Social Security numbers**
8. **Medical record numbers**
9. **Health plan beneficiary numbers**
10. **Account numbers**
11. **Certificate/license numbers**
12. **Vehicle identifiers and serial numbers**
13. **Device identifiers and serial numbers**
14. **Web URLs**
15. **IP addresses**
16. **Biometric identifiers** (fingerprints, voiceprints)
17. **Full-face photographs and comparable images**
18. **Any other unique identifying characteristic or code**

### De-identification for Neuropsych Reports

**Safe Harbor Method:**
- Remove all 18 identifiers
- Ages over 89 must be aggregated to "90 or older"
- Dates can keep year only
- Geographic areas can include state only
- Rare conditions or unique injury circumstances may be identifying even with identifiers removed — use clinical judgment

**Special Considerations for Neuropsych:**
- Detailed injury narratives (e.g., specific accident descriptions) may be identifying
- Rare diagnoses with small populations increase re-identification risk
- School names, employer names, and treating clinician names are indirect identifiers
- Legal case details may be uniquely identifying

### Minimum Necessary Standard

Neuropsychological reports should include only information necessary for the stated purpose:
- **Treatment referrals:** Full report typically appropriate
- **School accommodations:** Focus on cognitive profile, functional impact, and specific recommendations; psychiatric/substance history may be extraneous
- **Employer/disability:** Include functional capacity; limit irrelevant personal history
- **Legal/forensic:** Scope defined by psycholegal question; document everything reviewed

### Patient Authorization for Release

**Required for neuropsychological reports because they go beyond routine TPO:**
- Specific description of information to be released
- Who is authorized to release
- Who will receive the information
- Purpose of disclosure
- Expiration date or event
- Patient signature and date
- Right to revoke
- Warning about potential re-disclosure

**Special neuropsych considerations:**
- Separate authorization may be needed for raw test data vs. the report
- Psychotherapy notes (if applicable) have heightened HIPAA protections
- Forensic evaluations have different confidentiality frameworks (see below)

## APA Ethical Standards for Neuropsychological Assessment

### Standard 2.01 — Competence

- Practice within boundaries of competence based on education, training, supervised experience, and professional experience
- Neuropsychological evaluation requires specialized postdoctoral training
- Board certification (ABPP/ABCN) is the gold standard but not legally required in most states

### Standard 3.10 — Informed Consent

Before evaluation, inform the patient/guardian of:
- Purpose and nature of the evaluation
- Who requested the evaluation
- Who will receive the report
- Anticipated use of findings
- Limits of confidentiality
- Whether this constitutes a treatment relationship
- Right to decline (if not court-ordered)
- Fee structure and payment responsibility

**Forensic informed consent adds:**
- That the examiner is not the patient's treatment provider
- That the evaluation is being conducted for a third party
- That the patient does not have the same confidentiality protections as in treatment

### Standard 9.01 — Bases for Assessments

- Base opinions on sufficient information and appropriate techniques
- Do not diagnose from insufficient data
- If evaluation is limited (e.g., by patient cooperation, time constraints, validity failures), state limitations clearly

### Standard 9.02 — Use of Assessments

- Use tests appropriate to the purpose and population
- Consider validity and reliability for the specific patient
- Account for linguistic, cultural, and individual differences that may affect test interpretation
- Do not base conclusions on obsolete or outdated test instruments

### Standard 9.03 — Informed Consent in Assessments

- Inform the person being assessed of:
  - The nature and purpose of assessment
  - Fees
  - Involvement of third parties
  - Limits of confidentiality
- For mandated or court-ordered evaluations, inform of the nature of the evaluation and anticipated use

### Standard 9.04 — Release of Test Data

**Test Data** (raw scores, patient responses, notes about behavior) vs. **Test Materials** (manuals, test items, stimuli, scoring keys):

- **Test data** may be released to the patient or their designee upon valid authorization
- **Test materials** are protected and should NOT be released to non-psychologists
- When releasing data to attorneys, courts, or non-psychologists:
  - May release test data (scores, responses) with valid authorization or court order
  - Should NOT release test materials (items, manuals, scoring keys)
  - Consider protective order for test materials in legal proceedings

### Standard 9.06 — Interpreting Assessment Results

Account for:
- Purpose of assessment
- Various test factors and test-taking abilities
- Situational, personal, linguistic, and cultural differences
- Do not interpret results of tests you did not administer unless clearly stated

### Standard 9.11 — Maintaining Test Security

- Do NOT include actual test items, stimuli, or specific prompts in reports
- Do NOT reproduce copyrighted test materials in reports
- DO include: test names, scores, normative classifications, qualitative descriptions of performance, error patterns
- In legal proceedings, advocate for protective orders to prevent public disclosure of test materials

## Test Security in Neuropsychological Reports

### What to Include

✅ Test names and versions
✅ Standard scores, scaled scores, T-scores, percentiles
✅ Normative classifications (e.g., "Average," "Impaired")
✅ Qualitative descriptions of performance patterns
✅ Error types and categories (e.g., "intrusion errors," "perseverative responses")
✅ Learning curves and retention rates (e.g., "recalled 12/16 words after delay")
✅ General descriptions (e.g., "difficulty with tasks requiring cognitive flexibility")

### What NOT to Include

❌ Specific test items or prompts (e.g., "When asked to define 'sanctuary'...")
❌ Verbatim patient responses that reveal test content
❌ Test stimuli or images
❌ Scoring criteria or algorithms
❌ Specific items missed (e.g., "could not name 'asparagus' on BNT")
❌ Reproduction of copyrighted test forms

### Gray Areas

⚠️ Clock Drawing Test reproductions — acceptable if the test is commonly published; use clinical judgment
⚠️ Rey Complex Figure Test copy — the figure is widely published; reproduction in clinical reports is generally acceptable
⚠️ Specific CVLT-3 word lists — do NOT include; instead describe patterns (e.g., "semantic clustering strategy")

## Forensic-Specific Ethical Considerations

### Role Clarification

The evaluator must clearly define their role:
- **Treating clinician** — primary duty to the patient
- **Independent examiner** — retained by one party; no treatment relationship
- **Court-appointed expert** — neutral evaluator appointed by the court

### Daubert/Frye Standards

Neuropsychological testimony and reports used in legal proceedings must meet admissibility standards:

**Daubert Standard (federal and most states):**
1. Is the theory or technique testable/falsifiable?
2. Has it been subjected to peer review and publication?
3. What is the known or potential error rate?
4. Are there standards controlling the technique's operation?
5. Is there general acceptance within the relevant scientific community?

**Frye Standard (some states):**
- Is the methodology generally accepted by the relevant scientific community?

**Implications for neuropsychological reports:**
- Use validated, peer-reviewed tests with established norms
- Apply published cutoffs for validity measures
- Base diagnostic conclusions on established diagnostic criteria (DSM-5-TR)
- Cite supporting literature for opinions when appropriate
- Acknowledge limitations and alternative explanations

### Malingering Assessment

In forensic contexts, explicitly address:
- Results of performance validity tests (PVTs)
- Results of symptom validity tests (SVTs)
- Consistency of presentation with known neurocognitive patterns
- Consistency of reported symptoms with test performance
- Response to standard validity measure cutoffs
- Base rates of validity test failure in clinical vs. litigating populations

### Third-Party Observers

- APA and NAN position: third-party observers (including attorneys) should NOT be present during neuropsychological testing
- Observation can alter test performance and invalidate normative comparisons
- If observation is mandated by court, document its potential impact on validity
- Audio/video recording has similar concerns

## Record Retention

### General Guidelines

- Retain neuropsychological records for the period required by state law (typically 7 years after last contact for adults; until age of majority + state retention period for minors)
- Some states require longer retention
- Federal regulations may apply for certain settings (VA, military, federally funded research)

### What to Retain

- Signed informed consent
- Complete test protocols and response forms
- Raw data and scoring
- Final report
- Correspondence related to the evaluation
- Authorization for release forms
- Billing records
- Notes from clinical interview

### Destruction

- Shred paper records
- Securely delete electronic records
- Document destruction in a log
- Do NOT destroy records that are subject to litigation hold or subpoena

## Good Documentation Practice (GDP) for Neuropsych

### ALCOA-CCEA Principles Applied to Neuropsych

- **Attributable:** All entries signed/initialed by the evaluator; indicate who administered each test
- **Legible:** Readable scoring and notes; typed reports preferred
- **Contemporaneous:** Score during or immediately after administration; write report promptly
- **Original:** Retain original test protocols; copies are not substitutes
- **Accurate:** Double-check scoring; verify score conversions; proofread reports
- **Complete:** All administered tests documented; all scores reported; all sections completed
- **Consistent:** No discrepancies between score tables and narrative; consistent use of classification terms
- **Enduring:** Records maintained for required retention period in durable format
- **Available:** Records accessible for review, audit, or legal proceedings

### Corrections

- Never alter original test protocols or reports without documentation
- Use addenda for corrections or updates to finalized reports
- Date and sign all addenda
- State reason for correction
- Never obliterate original entries
