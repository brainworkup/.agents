# Diagnostic Frameworks for Neuropsychological Evaluation

Quick reference for DSM-5-TR diagnostic criteria commonly applied in neuropsychological evaluations.

---

## Neurocognitive Disorders (NCD)

### Major Neurocognitive Disorder (DSM-5-TR)

**Criteria:**
A. Evidence of **significant** cognitive decline from a previous level of performance in one or more cognitive domains (complex attention, executive function, learning and memory, language, perceptual-motor, or social cognition) based on:
   1. Concern of the individual, a knowledgeable informant, or the clinician that there has been a significant decline; AND
   2. A **substantial** impairment in cognitive performance, preferably documented by standardized neuropsychological testing or, in its absence, another quantified clinical assessment (typically ≥ 2 SD below appropriate norms)

B. The cognitive deficits **interfere with independence** in everyday activities (at minimum, assistance needed with complex instrumental ADLs)

C. Not exclusively in the context of delirium

D. Not better explained by another mental disorder

**Specify etiology:** Alzheimer's disease, frontotemporal lobar degeneration, Lewy body disease, vascular disease, traumatic brain injury, substance/medication-induced, HIV infection, prion disease, Parkinson's disease, Huntington's disease, another medical condition, multiple etiologies, unspecified

**Specify:** With or without behavioral disturbance (specify type)
**Specify current severity:** Mild, Moderate, Severe

---

### Mild Neurocognitive Disorder (DSM-5-TR)

**Criteria:**
A. Evidence of **modest** cognitive decline from a previous level in one or more domains based on:
   1. Concern of the individual, informant, or clinician; AND
   2. A **modest** impairment in cognitive performance documented by testing (typically 1–2 SD below appropriate norms)

B. The cognitive deficits **do NOT interfere with independence** in everyday activities, but greater effort, compensatory strategies, or accommodation may be needed

C. Not exclusively in the context of delirium

D. Not better explained by another mental disorder

**Same specifiers as Major NCD**

---

### Etiological Profiles

| Etiology | Typical Cognitive Profile | Key Features |
|----------|--------------------------|-------------|
| **Alzheimer's** | Amnesia (encoding deficit with rapid forgetting), then language, visuospatial, executive | Insidious onset, gradual progression; hippocampal atrophy on MRI |
| **Vascular** | Executive dysfunction, processing speed; variable memory (retrieval > encoding) | Stepwise or fluctuating course; white matter disease, lacunar infarcts |
| **Lewy Body** | Visuospatial, attention fluctuations, executive dysfunction | Visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, REM sleep behavior disorder, sensitivity to antipsychotics |
| **Frontotemporal (bvFTD)** | Executive dysfunction, behavioral disinhibition; memory relatively preserved early | Personality change, apathy, loss of empathy, compulsive behaviors, hyperorality |
| **Frontotemporal (PPA)** | Language predominant (nonfluent/agrammatic, semantic, logopenic) | Progressive aphasia; behavior/memory relatively preserved early |
| **TBI** | Attention, processing speed, executive, memory (encoding and retrieval) | Clear temporal relationship to injury; may improve over time |
| **Parkinson's** | Executive dysfunction, visuospatial, processing speed; memory retrieval > encoding | Motor symptoms precede cognitive decline; subcortical profile |

## Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

### DSM-5-TR Criteria

A. A persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development:

**Inattention (≥ 6 for children; ≥ 5 for age 17+):**
1. Fails to give close attention / careless mistakes
2. Difficulty sustaining attention
3. Does not seem to listen when spoken to directly
4. Does not follow through on instructions / fails to finish
5. Difficulty organizing tasks and activities
6. Avoids tasks requiring sustained mental effort
7. Loses things necessary for tasks
8. Easily distracted by extraneous stimuli
9. Forgetful in daily activities

**Hyperactivity-Impulsivity (≥ 6 for children; ≥ 5 for age 17+):**
1. Fidgets or squirms
2. Leaves seat when remaining seated is expected
3. Runs/climbs in inappropriate situations (restlessness in adults)
4. Unable to play or engage in leisure activities quietly
5. "On the go" or "driven by a motor"
6. Talks excessively
7. Blurts out answers
8. Difficulty waiting turn
9. Interrupts or intrudes on others

B. Several symptoms present **prior to age 12 years**
C. Present in **two or more settings**
D. Clear evidence of functional interference
E. Not better explained by another mental disorder

**Presentations:**
- Combined (314.01 / F90.2)
- Predominantly Inattentive (314.00 / F90.0)
- Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive (314.01 / F90.1)

**Neuropsychological Considerations:**
- ADHD is a clinical diagnosis; no single test confirms or rules out ADHD
- CPT alone is insufficient; sensitivity ~60–70%
- Look for convergent evidence: history, rating scales, behavioral observations, cognitive testing
- Executive dysfunction on testing supports but is not required
- Processing speed and working memory weaknesses are common but not diagnostic

## Specific Learning Disorder (SLD)

### DSM-5-TR Criteria

A. Difficulties learning and using academic skills, with at least one symptom persisting for ≥ 6 months despite targeted intervention:
1. Inaccurate or slow/effortful word reading
2. Difficulty understanding meaning of what is read
3. Difficulties with spelling
4. Difficulties with written expression
5. Difficulties mastering number sense, number facts, or calculation
6. Difficulties with mathematical reasoning

B. Affected academic skills substantially and quantifiably **below age expectations** and cause significant interference with functioning

C. Learning difficulties begin during school-age years (may not fully manifest until demands exceed capacity)

D. Not better accounted for by intellectual disabilities, sensory deficits, other mental/neurological disorders, psychosocial adversity, lack of instruction, or language proficiency

**Specify area:**
- With impairment in reading (315.00 / F81.0) — *"dyslexia"*
- With impairment in written expression (315.2 / F81.81)
- With impairment in mathematics (315.1 / F81.2) — *"dyscalculia"*

**Specify severity:** Mild, Moderate, Severe

**Neuropsychological Considerations:**
- Identify cognitive processing weaknesses underlying academic deficits
- Achievement-IQ discrepancy model is no longer required
- Pattern of Strengths and Weaknesses (PSW) approach is commonly used
- Phonological processing deficits are hallmark of dyslexia
- Rule out ADHD, anxiety, depression, sensory deficits as primary cause

## Intellectual Disability (ID)

### DSM-5-TR Criteria (Intellectual Developmental Disorder)

A. Deficits in intellectual functions (reasoning, problem-solving, planning, abstract thinking, judgment, academic learning, learning from experience) confirmed by clinical assessment AND standardized intelligence testing (IQ approximately ≤ 70 ± 5, SEM)

B. Deficits in adaptive functioning that limit functioning in one or more activities of daily life (communication, social participation, independent living) across multiple environments

C. Onset during the developmental period

**Severity based on adaptive functioning (not IQ):**
- Mild (317 / F70)
- Moderate (318.0 / F71)
- Severe (318.1 / F72)
- Profound (318.2 / F73)

---

## Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

### DSM-5-TR Criteria

A. Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple contexts:
1. Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
2. Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors
3. Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships

B. Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities (≥ 2 of 4):
1. Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech
2. Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines
3. Highly restricted, fixated interests
4. Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input

C. Symptoms present in early developmental period

D. Symptoms cause clinically significant impairment

E. Not better explained by intellectual disability or global developmental delay

**Specify severity level (1–3) for each domain** (social communication and restricted/repetitive behaviors):
- Level 1: Requiring support
- Level 2: Requiring substantial support
- Level 3: Requiring very substantial support

**Specify:** With or without intellectual impairment, language impairment, associated medical/genetic condition, another NDD/mental/behavioral disorder, catatonia

## Postconcussional Syndrome / Persistent Post-Concussive Symptoms

**ICD-10 F07.81 / DSM-5-TR: see Mild NCD due to TBI**

**Common symptom clusters:**
- Cognitive: attention, memory, processing speed
- Physical: headache, dizziness, fatigue, photophobia, phonophobia
- Emotional: irritability, anxiety, depression, emotional lability
- Sleep: insomnia, hypersomnia

**Neuropsychological Considerations:**
- Most concussions resolve within 1–3 months
- Persistent symptoms (>3 months) warrant comprehensive evaluation
- Must differentiate cognitive deficits from emotional/psychiatric contributions
- Performance validity testing is essential
- Consider pre-injury factors (prior concussions, psychiatric history, learning disabilities)
- Litigation/secondary gain context requires heightened validity assessment

## Depressive Disorders — Cognitive Impact

**Major Depressive Disorder can produce:**
- Psychomotor slowing (processing speed)
- Attention/concentration difficulties
- Working memory weakness
- Executive dysfunction (planning, initiation)
- Subjective memory complaints (often > objective deficits)
- Pseudodementia presentation in older adults

**Key differentiation from NCD:**
- Depression: Effortful processing affected; recognition memory intact; "I don't know" responses; consistent subjective complaints; potentially reversible with treatment
- NCD: Encoding and storage deficits; recognition may be impaired; confabulation possible; may minimize deficits; progressive course

---

## Anxiety Disorders — Cognitive Impact

**Anxiety can produce:**
- Attention/concentration difficulties (internal distraction)
- Working memory interference
- Processing speed reduction (overthinking)
- Test anxiety effects (underperformance on timed measures)
- Subjective cognitive complaints disproportionate to objective findings
