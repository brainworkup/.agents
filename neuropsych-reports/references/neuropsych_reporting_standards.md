# Neuropsychological Reporting — Expectations Summary

This document is an **original summary** of widely accepted professional expectations for **written neuropsychological evaluation reports**. It is not a substitute for jurisdiction-specific rules, institutional templates, test publishers’ reporting guidance, or supervision. **Do not copy** proprietary manual text or paywalled guidelines into reports; use publisher-authorized score labels and interpretive ranges.

---

## 1. Purpose and Audience

Reports should make the **referral question** explicit and answer it with **data-supported reasoning** understandable to **intended readers** (treaters, educators, insurers, attorneys, or patients/caregivers), without unnecessary jargon or unexplained scores.

---

## 2. Transparency of Methods

Professional readers expect:

- **Who** was evaluated, **when**, and under what **conditions** (language, sensory aids, breaks, fatigue).
- **What** was administered: **test names, editions/forms**, and **normative frame** (e.g., age-corrected as per manual).
- **Collateral** sources when used (caregivers, records) and **limitations** when collateral is absent.

---

## 3. Validity and Quality of Data

When the battery includes **performance validity** or **symptom validity** measures, or when data suggest variable effort or over-reporting, reports typically:

- Summarize **relevant validity indicators** in dedicated or integrated narrative.
- State **how validity affects confidence** in domain interpretations.
- Use **neutral, non-pejorative** wording consistent with professional ethics.

---

## 4. Structure and Integration

Strong reports usually:

- Separate **observation** from **interpretation**.
- Organize findings by **cognitive domains** and **functional implications**, not only by test name.
- Provide **integration** linking history, imaging (if any), psychiatric factors, and test patterns.
- Include **differential diagnostic** reasoning when appropriate to referral.
- End with **specific, actionable recommendations** tied to findings.

---

## 5. Forensic and High-Stakes Contexts

When reports may be used in **legal or quasi-legal** settings, additional expectations often include:

- Clear **scope, purpose, and role** (e.g., independent evaluation).
- **Materials reviewed** and **questions addressed** (and those explicitly not addressed).
- Distinction between **facts** and **professional opinions**.
- **Alternative hypotheses** and **limits** of methods and conclusions.

Exact rules vary by jurisdiction and retaining instructions; attorneys and supervisors should review.

---

## 6. Cultural, Linguistic, and Equity Considerations

Reports should acknowledge **language of assessment**, **appropriateness of norms**, **acculturation**, **education quality**, and **sensory/physical barriers** when they affect interpretation, and should **qualify conclusions** accordingly.

---

## 7. Pediatric vs Adult

**Pediatric** reports typically foreground **developmental context**, **caregiver and teacher input**, **school services**, and **developmentally normed** instruments. **Adult** reports may emphasize **vocational capacity**, **independence**, and **premorbid estimation** when inferring change.

---

## Public and Professional References (for further reading)

Use current versions and institutional access as available:

1. **American Academy of Clinical Neuropsychology (AACN)** — published practice guidelines, position papers, and consensus statements on clinical neuropsychology practice (including assessment and professional issues). Website: [https://www.theaacn.org/](https://www.theaacn.org/)

2. **National Academy of Neuropsychology (NAN)** — professional resources and publications relevant to practice and ethics. Website: [https://nanonline.org/](https://nanonline.org/)

3. **American Psychological Association (APA)** — **Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct**; **Record Keeping Guidelines**; division resources for neuropsychology (Division 40). Website: [https://www.apa.org/](https://www.apa.org/)

4. **Specialty training context** — Historical consensus on specialty training in clinical neuropsychology (often cited as the “Houston Conference” literature) informs **competency** expectations for who should conduct evaluations; see peer-reviewed archives such as *Archives of Clinical Neuropsychology* for the original conference materials and updates.

5. **Test-specific manuals** — Score labels, confidence intervals, reliability, and interpretive guidance are **publisher-defined**; reports should align terminology with each instrument’s manual and updates.
