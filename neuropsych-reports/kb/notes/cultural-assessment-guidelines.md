# Cross-Cultural Assessment Guidelines

> Guidelines for neuropsychological evaluation of culturally and linguistically diverse patients, including test selection, interpreter use, norm considerations, and required report documentation.

---

## Core Principles

1. **All neuropsychological tests are culturally loaded** — no test is "culture-free"
2. **Normative samples may not represent the patient** — evaluate before interpreting
3. **Low scores may reflect cultural/linguistic factors, not impairment**
4. **The clinician bears the burden of justifying interpretation**

---

## Pre-Evaluation

- Document primary language, language of education, daily use language
- Assess academic/cognitive language proficiency (not just conversational)
- Consider education quality (years ≠ quality)
- Evaluate acculturation and test familiarity

---

## Interpreter Use

- Trained medical interpreter preferred over family members
- Interpreter should NOT simplify, explain, or rephrase test items
- Some tests are invalidated by interpretation (verbal fluency, language tests)
- Document interpreter presence and impact on validity

---

## Required Report Documentation

1. Language of evaluation and patient proficiency
2. Interpreter use (if applicable) and impact
3. Norms used and representativeness
4. Cultural/linguistic factors affecting performance
5. Caveats about interpretation

---

## Common Pitfalls

- Assuming bilingual = proficient in test language
- Using family members as interpreters
- Applying U.S.-normed tests to non-U.S.-educated individuals without caveat
- Over-diagnosing impairment from culturally loaded tests
- Under-diagnosing by attributing all low scores to "cultural factors"
- Treating "nonverbal" tests as culture-free
