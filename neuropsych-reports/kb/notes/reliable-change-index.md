# Reliable Change Index (RCI)

> Statistical method for determining whether a change in test scores between two evaluations represents true change beyond measurement error and practice effects. Essential for serial neuropsychological assessments.

## Related Topics
- [[base-rates-low-scores]]

---

## General Guidelines

- Change of ≥ 1.0 SD (15 SS points) typically exceeds RCI at 90% confidence
- Consult **test-specific RCI tables** (available in most test manuals)
- Account for **practice effects** (especially TMT, Stroop, CVLT)
- **Regression to the mean** affects extreme initial scores

---

## Reporting Format

```
Domain              Baseline SS   Current SS   Difference   Exceeds RCI?
FSIQ                98            95           -3           No (WNL)
VCI                 105           102          -3           No (WNL)
WMI                 92            78           -14          Yes (Decline)*
PSI                 88            82           -6           No (WNL)
CVLT-3 LDFR (T)     45            30           -15          Yes (Decline)*

*Change exceeds the 90% confidence interval for reliable change, accounting
for practice effects (Iverson, 2001).
```

---

## Key Considerations

1. **Practice effects:** Most tests show improvement on retest; decline is more clinically meaningful than lack of improvement
2. **Retest interval:** Shorter intervals have larger practice effects; longer intervals may show regression
3. **Test-retest reliability:** Varies by test; some measures have poor reliability for change
4. **Confidence level:** 90% CI is standard; 95% is more conservative
5. **Clinical significance:** Statistical change may not be functionally meaningful — connect to real-world impact

---

## Factors That Complicate RCI

- Different norm sets between evaluations
- Different test versions (e.g., WAIS-IV → WAIS-V)
- Changes in medication, mood, or medical status
- Practice effects from non-test exposures (e.g., cognitive therapy)
